    Section 8.6 Quantifying the accuracy of approximations

    Motivating Questions

    • For an infinite series that converges, how much accuracy do we lose when we truncate the series after a finite number of terms to approximate its value with a finite sum? Said differently, to get a good approximation with a finite sum, how many terms are enough?
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • What is an alternating series and how can we determine whether or not it converges? How do the partial sums of a converging alternating series accurately estimate its exact sum?
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • What is the Lagrange Error Bound and how can we use it to understand how accurate a functionâ€™s degree-\(n\) Taylor polynomial approximation is?
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    This section is all about understanding precisely how accurate certain approximations are. Through Taylor series, we now know that we can represent certain functions and their values exactly through infinite sums. For example, we know that for any value of \(x\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} e^x = 1 + x + \frac{1}{2!}x^2 + \frac{1}{3!}x^3 + \cdots + \frac{1}{n!}x^n + \cdots \end{equation*}
    so it follows that
    \begin{equation} e^{-1/2} = 1 + \left(\frac{-1}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{2!} \left(\frac{-1}{2}\right)^2 + \cdots + \frac{1}{n!} \left(\frac{-1}{2}\right)^n + \cdots\text{.}\tag{8.6.1} \end{equation}
    If we decide to estimate the exact value of \(e^{-1/2}\) using the first \(5\) terms of this infinite sum, it turns out to be possible to use both the fact that we chose â€œ\(5\)â€ and properties of \(f(x) = e^x\) to quantify the maximum error in the estimate
    \begin{equation*} e^{-1/2} \approx 1 + \left(\frac{-1}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{2!} \left(\frac{-1}{2}\right)^2 + \frac{1}{3!} \left(\frac{-1}{2}\right)^3 + \frac{1}{4!} \left(\frac{-1}{2}\right)^4\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    As we have seen, most of the infinite series that arise (such as the one in EquationÂ (8.6.1)) are not geometric. But since geometric series are among the easiest infinite series to understand and evaluate, we use a geometric series as the starting point for our analysis of approximation errors. In Preview ActivityÂ 8.6.1, we investigate an example of a convergent geometric series and explore how we can use terms in the series itself to quantify how accurate a partial sum approximation is.

    Subsection 8.6.1 Alternating series of real numbers

    In several situations weâ€™ve encountered, series whose terms alternate in sign arise naturally. For instance, consider the definite integral
    \begin{equation*} \int_0^1 e^{-x^2} \, dx\text{,} \end{equation*}
    which is related to the well-known error function, \(\erf(x)\text{.}\) While we are unable to find an elementary algebraic antiderivative of \(e^{-x^2}\text{,}\) if we use the Taylor series
    \begin{equation*} e^{-x^2} = 1 - x^2 + \frac{1}{2!}x^4 - \frac{1}{3!}x^6 + \cdots + (-1)^n \frac{1}{n!} x^{2n} + \cdots \end{equation*}
    and apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus to the series representation of \(e^{-x^2}\text{,}\) we find that
    \begin{align} \int_0^1 e^{-x^2} \, dx =\mathstrut \amp \int_0^1 \left( 1 - x^2 + \frac{1}{2!}x^4 - \frac{1}{3!}x^6 + \cdots + (-1)^n \frac{1}{n!} x^{2n} + \cdots \right) \, dx\notag\\ =\mathstrut \amp \left. x - \frac{1}{3}x^3 + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} x^5 + \cdots + \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1) n!} x^{2n+1} + \cdots \right|_0^1 \notag\\ =\mathstrut \amp \left(1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} + \cdots + \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1) n!} + \cdots \right) - \left( 0 \right)\notag\\ =\mathstrut \amp 1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 2!} - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 3!} + \cdots + \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1) n!} + \cdots\tag{8.6.2} \end{align}
    Like the geometric series \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^k \left( \frac{4}{5} \right)^k \) that we encountered in Preview ActivityÂ 8.6.1, the infinite series in EquationÂ (8.6.2) is an example of an alternating series of real numbers. It turns out to be straightforward to determine whether or not an alternating series converges and to estimate the value of a convergent alternating series.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 8.6.2.

    An alternating series is a series of the form
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^k a_k\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(a_k \gt 0\) for each \(k\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    In Preview ActivityÂ 8.6.1, we investigated the partial sums of the convergent alternating geometric series \(S = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^k \left( \frac{4}{5} \right)^k\text{,}\) whose sum is \(S = \fra{5}{9}\text{.}\) We found the first several partial sums shown in 8.6.3. Plotting these partial sums in FigureÂ 8.6.4, we see an important pattern in how the partial sums approach the exact value of the infinite sum. In both the table and the figure, we see how consecutive partial sums oscillate back and forth above and below the exact sum of the infinite series, \(\frac{5}{9}\text{,}\) and moreover how the amount the next partial sum lies above or below \(\frac{5}{9}\) is less than the amount by which the previous partial sum deviated. For instance,
    \begin{equation*} |S_5 - \frac{5}{9}| \approx 0.1820, \end{equation*}
    but
    \begin{equation*} |S_6 - \frac{5}{9}| \approx 0.1456\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Moreover, since \(S_6 = S_5 - \left( \frac{4}{5} \right)^5\text{,}\) and \(-\left( \frac{4}{5} \right)^5 = -0.32768\) is the last and smallest term (in absolute value) in \(S_6\text{,}\) \(0.32768\) is the total vertical distance from the point \((5, S_5)\) to \((6, S_6)\) in FigureÂ 8.6.4. This implies that \(0.32768\) is more than the error in comparing \(S_5\) and \(S\text{.}\) Said differently, we are guaranteed that
    \begin{equation*} |S - S_5| \lt \left( \frac{4}{5} \right)^5 \end{equation*}
    so the next term in the sum provides a bound on the error in a given partial sum. A similar argument can be made for any value of \(n\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    A more general version of this argument can be made for any alternating series whose terms go to zero. This leads us to state two important results.
    ðŸ”—

    The Alternating Series Test.

    Given an alternating series
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^k a_k\text{,} \end{equation*}
    if the positive terms \(a_k\) decrease to 0 as \(k \to \infty\text{,}\) then the alternating series converges.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    If we were to compute the partial sums \(S_n\) of any alternating series whose terms decrease to zero and plot the points \((n,S_n)\) as we did in FigureÂ 8.6.4, we would see a similar picture: the partial sums alternate above and below the value to which the infinite alternating series converges. In addition, because the terms go to zero, the amount a given partial sum deviates from the total sum is at most the next term in the series. This result is formally stated as the Alternating Series Estimation Theorem.
    ðŸ”—

    Alternating Series Estimation Theorem.

    If the alternating series \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^{k}a_k\) has positive terms \(a_k\) that decrease to zero as \(k \to \infty\text{,}\) and \(S_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} (-1)^{k}a_k\) is the \(n\)th partial sum of the alternating series, then
    \begin{equation*} \left| \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^{k}a_k - S_n \right| \leq a_{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Again, this result simply says: if we use a partial sum to estimate the exact sum of a convergent alternating infinite series, the absolute error of the approximation is less than the next term in the series.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 8.6.5.

    Determine how well the \(100^{\mbox{th}}\) partial sum \(S_{100}\) of
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{k}}{k+1} \end{equation*}
    approximates the value of the converging alternating series.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    If we let \(S\) be the value of the series \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{k}}{k+1}\text{,}\) then we know that
    \begin{equation*} \left| S_{100} - S \right| \lt a_{100}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Now
    \begin{equation*} a_{100} = \frac{1}{101} \approx 0.0099\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so the \(100^{\mbox{th}}\) partial sum is within \(0.0099\) of the exact value of the series. In addition, it turns out that \(S_{100} \approx 0.688172\) and \(S = \ln(2) \approx 0.69314\text{,}\) so we see that the difference between \(S_{100}\) and \(S\) is indeed less than the error bound of \(0.0099\) from the Alternating Series Estimation Theorem.

    Subsection 8.6.2 Error Approximations for Taylor Polynomials

    In the same way that the next term in an alternating series reveals the maximum error of a finite approximation, it turns out that a quantity related to the next term in a Taylor series determines the maximum error found in a Taylor polynomial approximation. This will enable us to know what degree is Taylor polynomial is needed in order to achieve a given accuracy tolerance on a chosen interval.
    ðŸ”—
    Throughout what follows, we assume that \(f(x)\) is a function with at least \(n+1\) derivatives at \(a = 0\text{,}\) and let \(T_n(x)\) be the degree \(n\) Taylor polynomial of \(f\) centered at \(a = 0\text{.}\) We define the error function of the degree \(n\) approximation, \(E_n(x)\text{,}\) by
    \begin{equation*} E_n(x) = f(x) - T_n(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Our overall goal is to understand how much error there is in the approximation \(f(c) \approx T_n(c)\) for some fixed value of \(c\text{;}\) in other words, weâ€™d like to know the maximum possible value of \(E_n(c)\text{.}\) This leads us to maximize the error function, \(E_n(x)\text{,}\) on the interval \([0,c]\text{.}\) We observe several important properties of \(E_n(x)\text{:}\)
    • \(E_n(0) = E_n'(0) = E_n''(0) = \cdots = E_n^{(n)}(0) = 0\text{,}\) since \(E_n(x) = f(x) - T_n(x)\) and \(f\) and \(T_n\) share the same function and first \(n\) derivative values at \(a = 0\text{;}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • \(E^{(n+1)}_n(x) = f^{(n+1)}(x)\text{,}\) since \(T_n\) is a degree \(n\) polynomial, which means \(T_n^{(n+1)}(x) = 0\) for every \(x\text{;}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • If we assume that \(|f^{(n+1)}(x)| \leq M\) for some positive real number \(M\) for every \(x\) in the interval \([0,c]\text{,}\) then
      \begin{equation} | E^{(n+1)}_n(x) | \leq M\tag{8.6.3} \end{equation}
      since \(f^{(n+1)}(x) = E^{(n+1)}_n(x)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Taking InequalityÂ (8.6.3), writing it in the form \(-M \lt E^{(n+1)}_n(t) \leq M\text{,}\) and integrating all three terms in the inequality from \(t = 0\) to \(t = x\) and doing so \(n+1\) times, it can be shown that
    \begin{equation*} -M \frac{x^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \leq E_n(x) \leq M \frac{x^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \end{equation*}
    It follows that
    \begin{equation*} | E_n(x) | \leq M \frac{|x|^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \leq M \frac{|c|^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \end{equation*}
    since \(|c|\) is the maximum value of \(|x|\) on the interval \(0 \leq x \leq c\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    A similar argument works for the same result for any real number \(a\) and leads to the following result, known as the Lagrange Error Bound.
    ðŸ”—

    The Lagrange Error Bound for \(T_n(x)\).

    Let \(f\) be a continuous function with \(n+1\) continuous derivatives. Suppose that \(M\) is a positive real number such that \(\left|f^{(n+1)}(x)\right| \le M\) on the interval \([a, c]\text{.}\) If \(T_n(x)\) is the degree \(n\) Taylor polynomial for \(f(x)\) centered at \(x=a\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \left|T_n(c) - f(c)\right| \leq M\frac{|c-a|^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The Lagrange Error Bound shows that the total error in the degree \(n\) Taylor polynomial approximation is determined by the maximum value of the \((n+1)^{\mbox{st}}\) derivative of \(f\) on the interval of interest, together with the quantity \(\frac{|c-a|^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}\text{.}\) In practice, this provides a straightforward way to find a bound on the maximum error of a polynomial approximation of a non-polynomial function.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 8.6.6.

    Determine the maximum error possible when using the degree \(10\) Taylor polynomial centered at \(a = 0\text{,}\) \(T_{10}(x)\text{,}\) for \(\sin(x)\) to approximate the value of \(\sin(2)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    To approximate the value of \(\sin(2)\) using the degree \(10\) Taylor polynomial, we will use \(f(x) = \sin(x)\text{,}\) \(c = 2\text{,}\) \(a=0\text{,}\) and \(n = 10\) in the Lagrange Error Bound formula. We also need to find a value for \(M\) so that \(\left|f^{(11)}(x)\right| \le M\) on the interval \([0,2]\text{.}\) Since the derivatives of \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) are all one of \(\pm \sin(x)\) or \(\pm \cos(x)\text{,}\) and the maximum absolute values of these functions on any interval is \(1\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \left| f^{(n+1)}(x) \right| \leq 1 \end{equation*}
    for any \(n\) and any \(x\text{.}\) Therefore, we let \(M = 1\text{.}\) By the Lagrange Error Bound formula, we find that
    \begin{equation*} \left|T_{10}(2) - f(2)\right| \leq (1)\frac{|2-0|^{11}}{(11)!} = \frac{2^{11}}{(11)!} \approx 0.00005130671797\text{.} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    So \(T_{10}(2)\) approximates \(\sin(2)\) to within at most \(0.00005130671797\text{.}\) Using a computer algebra system, we can find that
    \begin{equation*} T_{10}(2) \approx 0.9093474427 \ \ \text{ and } \ \ \sin(2) \approx 0.9092974268 \end{equation*}
    with an actual difference of about \(0.0000500159\text{.}\) Note that the computer algebra system itself is using this same approach (but with more terms) to generate the fact that \(\sin(2) \approx 0.9092974268\text{.}\)

    Subsection 8.6.3 Summary

    • If we approximate an infinite series with one of its partial sums, we can often quantify the maximum error present in the truncated sum. Two ways the error can be measured are through the Alternating Series Estimation Theorem (if the original series is alternating) and through the Lagrange Error Bound (if the original series can be viewed as a Taylor series).
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • An alternating series is one whose terms alternate in sign, often represented by \(\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^{k}a_k\) where \(a_k \gt 0\) for all values of \(k\text{.}\) Any alternating series whose terms \(a_k\) approach zero as \(k \to \infty\) is guaranteed to converge. Moreover, the Alternating Series Estimation Theorem tells us that we can estimate the exact value of a converging alternating series by using a partial sum, and the error of that approximation is at most the next term in the series. That is,
      \begin{equation*} \left| \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^{k}a_k - \left(a_0 - a_1 + a_2 - a_3 + \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} a_{n-1} \right) \right| \lt a_{n}\text{.} \end{equation*}
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The Lagrange Error Bound quantifies the accuracy when we a Taylor polynomial to approximate a function. Specifically, if \(T_n(x)\) is the degree \(n\) order Taylor polynomial for \(f\) centered at \(x=a\) and if \(\left|f^{(n+1)}(x)\right| \leq M\) for some real number \(M\) on the interval \([a, c]\text{,}\) then
      \begin{equation*} \left|T_n(c) - f(c)\right| \leq M\frac{|c-a|^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}\text{.} \end{equation*}
