Example 8.4.4.

Explain why the Taylor series centered at \(a = 0\) for \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) is
\begin{equation*} T_f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^{k} \frac{1}{(2k+1)!}x^{2k+1} \end{equation*}
and find the interval of \(x\)-values for which this Taylor series converges. Investigate whether or not the Taylor series converges to \(f(x) = \sin(x)\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Solution.
We observed in SectionÂ 8.2 that the derivatives of the sine function cycle and repeat every \(4\) derivatives, since
\begin{equation*} f(x) = \sin(x), f'(x) = \cos(x), f''(x) = -\sin(x), f'''(x) = -\cos(x), \end{equation*}
and so
\begin{equation*} f^{(4)}(x) = \sin(x), f^{(5)}(x) = \cos(x), f^{(6)}(x) = -\sin(x), f^{(7)}(x) = -\cos(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}
More generally, \(f^{(4k)}(x) = \sin(x)\) and \(f^{(4k+2)}(x) = -\sin(x)\) for every natural number \(k\text{.}\) This shows that \(f^{(4k)}(0) = \sin(0) = 0\) and \(f^{(4k+2)}(0) = -\sin(0) = 0\text{,}\) from which it follows that every even power of \(x\) in the Taylor series of the sine function has a coefficient of \(0\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
For odd values of \(n\text{,}\) we can see that since \(f'(x) = \cos(x)\text{,}\) we also have \(f^{(5)}(x) = \cos(x)\text{,}\) \(f^{(9)}(x) = \cos(x)\text{,}\) and so on. From this, \(f'(0) = \cos(0) = 1\text{,}\) and similarly, \(f^{(5)}(0) = 1\text{,}\) \(f^{(9)}(x) = 1\text{,}\) and indeed \(f^{(4k+1)}(0) = 1\) for every nonnegative whole number \(k\text{.}\) Finally, since \(f'''(x) = -\cos(x)\text{,}\) we have \(f'''(0) = -\cos(0) = -1\text{,}\) and so \(f'''(0) = -1\text{,}\) \(f^{(7)}(0) = -1\text{,}\) and thus \(f^{(4k+3)}(0) = -1\) for every nonnegative whole number \(k\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Putting these observations together about the various derivatives of \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) evaluated at \(a = 0\) and using the fact that the Taylor series of \(f(x)\) centered at \(0\) has form
\begin{equation*} T_f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(k)}(0)}{k!}x^k \end{equation*}
we see that
\begin{align} T_f(x) =\mathstrut \amp x - \frac{1}{3!}x^3 + \frac{1}{5!}x^5 - \cdots + (-1)^{n} \frac{1}{(2n+1)!} x^{2n+1} + \cdots\notag\\ =\mathstrut \amp \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (-1)^{k} \frac{1}{(2k+1)!}x^{2k+1}\tag{8.4.3} \end{align}
ðŸ”—
Next, we use the Ratio Test to investigate the values of \(x\) where \(T_f(x)\) converges. We therefore consider \(r_n(x)\text{,}\) which is the ratio of consecutive nonzero terms, and have
\begin{equation*} r_n(x) = \frac{(-1)^{n+1} \frac{1}{(2(n+1)+1)!}x^{2(n+1)+1}}{(-1)^{n} \frac{1}{(2n+1)!}x^{2n+1}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Expanding the expression \(2(n+1)\text{,}\) combining like terms, and slightly rearranging how we write the fraction, we see that
\begin{align} r_n(x) =\mathstrut \amp \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{(-1)^{n}} \cdot \frac{\frac{1}{(2n+3)!}}{\frac{1}{(2n+1)!}} \cdot \frac{x^{2n+3}}{x^{2n+1}}\notag\\ =\mathstrut \amp \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{(-1)^{n}} \cdot \frac{(2n+1)!}{(2n+3)!} \cdot \frac{x^{2n+3}}{x^{2n+1}}\tag{8.4.4} \end{align}
We now consider the three fractions in EquationÂ (8.4.4) one at a time and simplify each. First, we observe that
\begin{equation*} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{(-1)^{n}} = -1 \end{equation*}
since the numerator and denominator are each always either \(1\) or \(-1\) and always of opposite signs. Next, we expand the factorials and see that
\begin{equation*} (2n+1)! = (2n+1) (2n) (2n-1) \cdots (2) (1) \end{equation*}
and
\begin{equation*} (2n+3)! = (2n+3) (2n+2) (2n+1) (2n) (2n-1) \cdots (2) (1) \end{equation*}
so
\begin{equation*} \frac{(2n+1)!}{(2n+3)!} = \frac{1}{(2n+3)(2n+2)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Finally, we observe that
\begin{equation*} \frac{x^{2n+3}}{x^{2n+1}} = x^{(2n+3) - (2n+1)} = x^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
Putting all of this work together, we can update EquationÂ (8.4.4) to the simpler form
\begin{equation*} r_n(x) = (-1) \cdot \frac{1}{(2n+3)(2n+2)} \cdot x^2\text{.} \end{equation*}
From here, we compute \(r(x)\text{,}\) the limit of \(r_n(x)\) as \(n\) increases without bound. Since \(\frac{1}{(2n+3)(2n+2)} \to 0\) as \(n \to \infty\text{,}\) it follows that for any fixed value of \(x\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} r(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} r_n(x) = \lim_{n \to \infty} (-1) \cdot \frac{1}{(2n+3)(2n+2)} \cdot x^2 = -1 \cdot 0 \cdot x^2 = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}
The Ratio Test tells us that for any \(x\) for which \(|r(x)| \lt 1\text{,}\) the series \(T_f(x)\) converges. Here, since \(r(x) = 0\) for every value of \(x\text{,}\) this means that \(T_f(x)\) converges for every real number \(x\text{.}\)
ðŸ”—
Finally, we investigate whether the Taylor series appears to converge to \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) or not. In FigureÂ 8.4.5, we see the Taylor polynomials of degree \(5\) and \(9\text{,}\) and the significant improvement of the approximation as the degree increases.
ðŸ”—
For the degree \(9\) approximation, we see that \(T_9(x)\) and \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) are almost indistinguishable on \(-4 \lt x \lt 4\text{,}\) which is much wider than the interval \(-2 \lt x \lt 2\) on which the \(T_5(x)\) approximation is accurate. Experimenting with even higher degree Taylor polynomials shows that increasing the degree of the approximation appears to widen the interval on which the approximation is accurate and seems to do so without bound, which suggests that the Taylor series of \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) in fact converges to \(f(x) = \sin(x)\text{.}\)
