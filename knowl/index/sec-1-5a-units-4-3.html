We sometimes write \(\frac{df}{dx}\) or \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) instead of \(f'(x)\text{,}\) and these alternate notations help us see the units and meaning of the derivative as the instantaneous rate of change of \(f\) with respect to \(x\). The units on the average rate of change of \(f\) on the interval \(AV_{[a,a+h]}\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} AV_{[a,a+h]} = \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}\text{,} \end{equation*}
are “units of \(f\) per unit of \(x\text{,}\)” and when we take the limit of the average rate of change as \(h\) goes to zero, the derivative
\begin{equation*} f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \end{equation*}
has the same units: units of \(f\) per unit of \(x\text{.}\) Said slightly differently, the units on the derivative function are “units of output per unit of input” for the variables of the original function, \(f\text{.}\)
